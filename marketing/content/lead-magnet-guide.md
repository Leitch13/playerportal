## Chapter 1: Getting Your First 50 Players

Here's the truth that nobody wants to hear: you don't need a fancy website, a professional logo, or a thousand Instagram followers to get your first 50 players. You need three things --- and I'm going to give them to you straight.

### The 3 Channels That Actually Work

When I started PlayIt Love It, I didn't have a marketing budget. I had about twenty quid and a lot of energy. Here's where my first players came from, and here's where yours will come from too.

**1. School Partnerships**

This is the single most powerful channel for a grassroots coaching business, and almost nobody uses it properly. Here's what I did: I walked into local primary schools in Aberdeen and offered to run free taster sessions during PE or after school. Not a sales pitch. Just genuinely good coaching sessions.

Think about it from the school's perspective. They're stretched thin. PE provision is often rubbish. If someone walks in and says "I'll run a quality football session for your pupils, free of charge," they'll bite your hand off. You deliver a belting session, the kids go home buzzing, and you hand out flyers for your own sessions at the end. That's it.

I built relationships with five schools in the first few months. That alone brought in over 30 players. Some of those schools still work with us years later.

**2. Local Facebook Groups**

Every area has them. "Aberdeen Parents," "Mums of Aberdeen," "What's On in Aberdeen for Kids." These groups are goldmines. Parents are constantly asking for activities for their children. You don't need to spam the group with adverts. Just post something genuine: "Hi, I'm John, I'm a football coach based in [area]. I'm starting up sessions for 5-8 year olds on Saturday mornings at [venue]. First session is free. Drop me a message if your wee one fancies it."

That's it. No hard sell. Just tell people you exist. When I posted in three local groups, I got 15 enquiries in a week. Cost me nothing.

One tip: don't just post and disappear. Stick around in the group. Reply to other parents' questions. Be helpful. When someone asks "any good activities for a 6-year-old in Dyce?" --- that's your moment. People buy from people they trust, and trust starts with being genuinely useful in a community, not just shouting about your own stuff.

**3. Word of Mouth**

This one's obvious, but people underestimate it. Your first players will talk. Their parents will talk. If you deliver great sessions and treat families well, word of mouth will do more for you than any advert ever could.

I made it easy by being brilliant with parents. I learned every kid's name within two sessions. I sent a quick message after their first session: "Great to have [kid's name] today, they did really well." That stuff matters. Parents remember it, and they tell other parents.

Here's a real example. One of our earliest families --- mum brought her lad along for a free trial on a Saturday morning. He was shy, barely spoke. By the end of the hour, he was laughing his head off with the other kids. I messaged the mum that evening saying he was great and we'd love to see him back. She signed up immediately and then told three other mums at the school gate on Monday. Within two weeks, I had four new players from one trial. That's word of mouth doing its thing. You can't buy that kind of marketing.

### The "First Session Free" Strategy

This is non-negotiable when you're starting out. Offer every new player their first session for free. No strings attached.

Here's why it works: parents are cautious. They don't know you. They don't know if their kid will like it. Asking them to pay upfront is a barrier. Removing that barrier gets kids through the door. And once a kid has had a great time at your session, the parent is going to sign them up. I converted about 80% of trial players into paying members.

The key is making that first session absolutely quality. Plan it properly. Make it fun. Make the kid feel welcome and like they belong. Don't just run a generic kickabout.

### How to Price Your First Sessions

I see new coaches making this mistake all the time: they go cheap. They think "I'll charge three quid a session so everyone can afford it." Mate, that's a race to the bottom. You'll run yourself into the ground and still not make enough to live on.

When I started, I charged five pounds per session. That was in Aberdeen, which isn't exactly Chelsea. Within six months I moved to six pounds, then seven. Now our sessions range from seven to ten pounds depending on the programme.

Parents don't want the cheapest option. They want good value. If your session is quality, they will pay a fair price. Price yourself too low and people actually trust you less --- they think there must be a catch.

My rule: look at what other activities in your area cost (swimming lessons, dance classes, gymnastics). Price yourself in line with them. You're a professional providing a service, not running a charity.

### Template: Your First Session Flyer

Here's the exact kind of thing you should put on a flyer or a social media post:

> **FUN FOOTBALL FOR KIDS AGED 5-8!**
>
> Every Saturday, 10am-11am at [Venue Name]
>
> Coached by [Your Name] (FA/SFA Qualified)
>
> All abilities welcome. First session FREE!
>
> Fun, safe, and quality coaching in a small group setting.
>
> Limited spaces --- message me on [phone/social] to book your free trial.

Keep it simple. Don't overthink it. The words "free," "fun," and "limited spaces" do the heavy lifting.

### Action Step: Do This TODAY

Pick three local primary schools within a 15-minute drive of your home. Find the head teacher's email on their website. Write a short, friendly email offering to run a free taster session. Send it today. Not tomorrow. Today. That one action could bring you 10 new players within a month.

## Chapter 2: Building Systems That Scale

Let me paint you a picture. You're coaching 60 players a week across four or five sessions. You're replying to parent messages at 11pm. You've got a spreadsheet for registers that's held together with hope. You're chasing payments through bank transfers and struggling to remember who's paid and who hasn't. You're exhausted.

Sound familiar? I lived this. And it's exactly why most grassroots coaches hit a wall at 50-80 players and either burn out or stay stuck there for years.

The difference between a coach who tops out at 60 players and one who grows to 200, 300, or more? Systems.

### Why Most Coaches Burn Out

It's not the coaching that kills you. It's the admin. Every player you add brings more messages, more payment chasing, more register keeping, more scheduling headaches. Without systems, your workload doesn't scale linearly --- it scales exponentially. Adding ten more players to a manual system doesn't add ten percent more work. It adds thirty percent.

I hit my breaking point around 70 players. I was coaching five days a week, doing all the admin in between, and I was shattered. I had no evenings off. I was making decent money but I had no life. Something had to change.

### The 4 Systems You Need

Here are the four core systems that transformed my business. You don't need all of them on day one, but you need to build towards them.

**1. Booking System**

Players and parents need a way to sign up that isn't "send me a WhatsApp." Early on, a Google Form works. As you grow, you need a proper booking system where parents can see available sessions, book a trial, and sign up. We eventually built our own platform for this (that's a story for another day), but there are plenty of off-the-shelf options.

**2. Payment System**

Stop chasing bank transfers. Stop accepting cash at sessions. Get a recurring payment system set up. GoCardless, Stripe, or a coaching platform that handles payments. When I switched from bank transfers to direct debits, my late payments dropped from about 30% to under 5%. That alone was life-changing.

**3. Communication System**

In the early days, a WhatsApp group for each session works fine. But as you grow, WhatsApp becomes chaos. You need a structured way to communicate with parents --- session updates, cancellations, schedule changes, events. Email works. A proper platform works better. The key is having one central channel, not five different WhatsApp groups you're trying to manage.

**4. Registers**

You need to know who's in each session, who attended, and who didn't. This matters for safeguarding, for tracking retention, and for knowing which sessions are full and which need more players. A paper register is fine for your first 30 players. After that, go digital. Even a simple spreadsheet is better than scraps of paper you lose in your coaching bag.

### When to Switch from Manual to Automated

Here's my rough guide:

- **0-30 players:** Manual is fine. Google Sheets, WhatsApp, bank transfers. Keep it simple and focus on coaching.
- **30-80 players:** Start automating payments and booking. This is where the admin starts eating your life.
- **80-150 players:** You need a proper platform. Automated everything. Your time should be spent on coaching and growing, not on spreadsheets.
- **150+ players:** If you're still doing things manually at this stage, you're either superhuman or on the verge of a breakdown. Systemise or die.

### Action Step

Audit your current setup. Write down every admin task you do in a typical week and how long each one takes. Identify the biggest time drain. That's the first thing you automate. For most coaches, it's payment collection. Sort that out first.

## Chapter 3: Pricing That Actually Works

Pricing is the thing coaches stress about more than anything else. Charge too much and you worry nobody will come. Charge too little and you can't pay your bills. I've tested pretty much every pricing model going, and I'm going to tell you what actually works.

### The Psychology of Pricing

Here's something that took me way too long to understand: price is not just a number. It's a signal. When a parent sees a session priced at three pounds, they think "how good can it be for three quid?" When they see it priced at eight pounds, they think "this must be decent."

I'm not saying charge a fortune. I'm saying charge what you're worth. If you're qualified, passionate, and delivering great sessions, eight to ten pounds per session for a grassroots programme is completely reasonable in most parts of the UK.

When I raised my prices from five to seven pounds, I didn't lose a single player. Not one. And my revenue went up forty percent overnight. I genuinely wish someone had told me that earlier. I spent months undercharging because I was scared of losing people, and all I was actually doing was undervaluing myself and my team.

### Block Booking vs Pay-As-You-Go vs Subscriptions

There are three main pricing models, and here's how they compare:

**Pay-As-You-Go:** The parent pays per session. Flexible for them, unpredictable for you. You never know how many kids are turning up each week, which makes planning a nightmare. I used this at the start and it was chaos.

**Block Booking:** Parents pay for a block of sessions upfront (usually 6-10 weeks). This is better. You get money upfront, which helps cash flow. Parents are more committed because they've already paid. This is what most grassroots coaches use and it works well.

**Subscriptions (Monthly Direct Debit):** This is the gold standard. Parents pay a fixed monthly amount and their child can attend their sessions each week. The parent gets simplicity. You get predictable, recurring income. When I switched to monthly subscriptions, my revenue became predictable for the first time ever. I could actually plan ahead.

My recommendation: start with block booking, then move to subscriptions once you have the systems to support it (see Chapter 2).

### How to Raise Prices Without Losing Players

You will need to raise your prices. Costs go up. Your coaching improves. Your programmes get better. Here's how to do it without a mass exodus:

1. **Give notice.** Tell parents at least 4-6 weeks before a price change.
2. **Explain the value.** "We're increasing our session fee from seven to eight pounds to fund new equipment, additional coaching staff, and improved programmes."
3. **Don't apologise.** State it matter-of-factly. You're running a business.
4. **Do it annually.** Small, regular increases are much easier to swallow than one big jump.

I raise our prices once a year, usually by fifty pence to a pound per session. I've never had more than one or two families leave because of a price increase.

### Real Numbers: What to Charge at Different Stages

These are rough guides based on my experience in Aberdeen. Adjust for your area.

- **Just starting out:** Five to six pounds per session
- **Established (50+ players):** Six to eight pounds per session
- **Premium programme (100+ players, branded, professional):** Eight to twelve pounds per session
- **Holiday camps:** Fifteen to twenty-five pounds per day
- **Intensive academies / development squads:** Forty to sixty pounds per month (subscription)

### The Value Ladder

This is the concept that really accelerated our growth. Instead of offering one thing at one price, you create a ladder:

1. **Free trial session** (entry point, zero risk)
2. **Block booking / regular sessions** (main product, seven to ten pounds per session)
3. **Monthly subscription** (higher commitment, better value per session)
4. **Premium programmes** (development squads, small group coaching, twelve to fifteen pounds per session)
5. **Holiday camps** (seasonal, higher price point)
6. **One-to-one coaching** (premium, twenty to thirty pounds per session)

Not every family will climb the whole ladder. But some will. And those who do become your most valuable, most loyal members.

### Action Step

If you're currently charging pay-as-you-go, switch to block booking for your next term. Set a price that's in line with other children's activities in your area. Don't go cheap. And add a free trial option if you don't already have one.

## Chapter 4: Marketing Without Being Salesy

I know, I know. You got into coaching because you love football, not because you wanted to become a marketer. I get it. I felt the same. But here's the reality: "build it and they'll come" does not work. If people don't know you exist, you don't have a business.

The good news? Marketing a coaching business doesn't have to be complicated or cringey. You don't need to be an influencer. You don't need to dance on TikTok. You just need to do a few simple things consistently.

### Content That Converts

Social media is the easiest free marketing tool you have. But most coaches either don't post at all, or they post the wrong things.

Here's what works:

- **Session clips:** Short videos of your sessions in action. Kids having fun, drills going well, goals being scored. Parents LOVE seeing their kids on your social media. And it shows potential new parents exactly what to expect.
- **Testimonials:** A screenshot of a parent's message saying how much their kid loves your sessions. This is the most powerful content you can post. Social proof is everything.
- **Your story:** People buy from people. Share why you coach. Share your journey. Share the ups and the downs. Be real and authentic.
- **Tips and value:** Quick coaching tips for parents, or football drills kids can do at home. This builds trust and positions you as an expert.

What doesn't work: generic stock photos, constant "BOOK NOW" posts, or sharing nothing at all.

### The Power of Parent Testimonials

I cannot overstate this. When a parent sends you a message saying their kid had an amazing time, screenshot it (with permission) and post it. When a parent leaves a Google review, share it. When a kid achieves something in your session, celebrate it publicly.

We have over 100 five-star Google reviews for PlayIt Love It. That didn't happen by accident. After a player's first month, I send a friendly message: "Hey, glad [kid's name] is enjoying the sessions! If you get a chance, a quick Google review would really help us out." Most parents are happy to do it.

Those reviews are now one of our biggest sources of new enquiries. When a parent in Aberdeen searches "kids football coaching," they find us, see 100+ five-star reviews, and the decision is basically made for them.

### Local Partnerships

Schools (covered in Chapter 1) are just the start. Think about who else your target audience interacts with:

- **Community centres:** Offer to run sessions at their venues. They get a new activity for their community, you get access to their audience.
- **Local sports shops:** Ask if you can leave flyers. Some will partner with you for mutual promotion.
- **Other children's activity providers:** Dance schools, swimming clubs, gymnastics classes. You're not competitors --- you serve the same parents. Cross-promote each other.
- **Local businesses:** Cafes near your venues, kids' clothing shops, soft play centres. Think about where parents go and get your name in front of them.

### Simple Facebook Ads That Work

When you're ready to invest a small budget in paid advertising, Facebook and Instagram ads are ridiculously effective for local coaching businesses. Here's the strategy I use:

- **Budget:** Five pounds per day. That's it. Thirty-five quid a week.
- **Target:** Parents aged 25-45, living within a 10-mile radius of your venue.
- **Ad content:** A short video of your sessions (30-60 seconds of kids having fun), with text overlay: "Fun football for kids aged 4-8. First session FREE. Book now."
- **Call to action:** Link to your booking page or a simple enquiry form.

At five pounds a day, I typically get 10-20 enquiries per week. If even half of those convert to trial sessions, and 80% of trials become members, that's 4-8 new players per week for the cost of a couple of coffees a day.

The maths speaks for itself.

One thing I'll say: don't overthink the creative. Your ad doesn't need to look like a Nike commercial. A slightly shaky phone video of kids laughing and playing football at your session, with a simple caption, will outperform a slick, over-produced advert every time. Real beats polished in this game. Parents want to see what their kid's experience will actually look like, not a Hollywood version of it.

### Action Step

If you're not posting on social media at least three times a week, start today. Film one quick clip at your next session (always get photo/video consent from parents first). Post it with a simple caption about what the kids worked on. That's it. Build the habit first.

## Chapter 5: Building a Team So You're Not Doing It All

This is the chapter that separates people who have a job from people who have a business. As long as you're the only one coaching, you don't have a business --- you have a self-employed gig where you can't take a day off.

I resisted hiring for way too long. I thought nobody could coach as well as me. I thought I couldn't afford it. I thought it would be too much hassle. I was wrong on all three counts.

### When to Hire Your First Coach

Here's my rule: hire before you think you're ready. If you're coaching more than four sessions a week and doing all the admin, you need help. Not next month. Now.

Your first hire doesn't have to be full-time. Start with a part-time assistant coach who takes one or two sessions a week. This frees you up to work on the business instead of just in it. That distinction is everything.

I hired my first coach when I had about 80 players. I should have done it at 50. Every month I delayed was a month I spent doing admin at midnight instead of growing the business.

### Where to Find Coaches

- **Local sports science / coaching students:** Universities and colleges are full of young people studying sport who would love the experience. They're enthusiastic, they're cheap, and many of them are great with kids.
- **Your own players' parents:** Sometimes a dad or mum at your sessions has coaching experience or badges. They already know your culture and your players.
- **SFA / FA coaching courses:** Connect with people on coaching courses. They're actively looking for experience and opportunities.
- **Word of mouth:** Tell people you're hiring. Post on social media. You'll be surprised who comes forward.

### Red Flags to Avoid

Not everyone who can coach is right for your business. Watch out for:

- People who are more interested in tactics than in making kids smile. At grassroots level, fun comes first. Always.
- People who can't commit reliably. If they miss sessions or turn up late, they'll damage your reputation.
- People who don't share your values. If your sessions are about development, fun, and inclusion, don't hire someone who's all about winning at any cost.
- People who won't take feedback. You need coaches who are coachable.

### How to Train and Retain Good Coaches

Finding a coach is one thing. Keeping them is another. Here's what I've learned:

1. **Invest in their development.** Pay for their coaching badges. Send them on courses. When coaches feel like they're growing, they stay.
2. **Pay fairly.** Don't take the mick. If your sessions charge eight pounds per player and you've got 15 kids in a session, you can afford to pay your coaches properly. I pay well above minimum wage and I've never regretted it.
3. **Give them ownership.** Let coaches run their own sessions their way (within your framework). Micromanagement kills motivation.
4. **Build a culture.** Regular team meetups. Clear communication. Celebrate wins together. Make them feel like they're part of something, not just hired help.

### The Progression

Think of your role as evolving through three stages:

1. **You coaching:** This is where everyone starts. You're on the pitch, delivering every session yourself. This is necessary at the start but not sustainable long-term.
2. **You managing:** You've hired coaches. You're still coaching some sessions, but you're also overseeing others, handling the business side, and driving growth. This is the transition phase.
3. **You leading:** You've built a team that can deliver without you on the pitch every day. You're focused on strategy, partnerships, new programmes, and the big picture. The business runs whether you're there or not.

I'm at stage three now. I still coach because I love it, not because the business needs me to. That's the difference. And that's the goal.

Getting to stage three didn't happen overnight. It took me a good couple of years of gradually stepping back from the pitch and building up my team. There were growing pains --- sessions that didn't go perfectly when I wasn't there, coaches who needed more support than I expected. But every time I pushed through that discomfort, the business came out stronger on the other side. The first time I took a full week's holiday and the business ran without a single issue, I knew I'd cracked it. That feeling is worth every difficult conversation and every moment of doubt along the way.

### Action Step

If you're doing everything yourself right now, write down the one session per week that you could hand to someone else. Start looking for that person today. Post on your social media: "Looking for an enthusiastic football coach to join our team. SFA/FA Level 1 preferred. DM me." You'll get replies.

## Conclusion

So there it is. Five steps. Nothing fancy. Nothing you need a business degree for. Just practical, proven stuff that I've done myself and that works.

Let me recap:

1. **Get your first 50 players** through school partnerships, local social media, and word of mouth. Use the "first session free" strategy and price yourself properly from day one.
2. **Build systems that scale** so you're not drowning in admin. Automate payments, bookings, and communication before they become a bottleneck.
3. **Price with confidence.** Block bookings, then subscriptions. Build a value ladder. Raise your prices annually and don't apologise for it.
4. **Market consistently** without being salesy. Social media, parent testimonials, local partnerships, and simple Facebook ads that cost less than your morning coffee.
5. **Build a team** so you have a business, not just a job. Hire before you're ready. Invest in your coaches. Move from coaching to managing to leading.

This is exactly what I did to build PlayIt Love It from a kickabout in the park to 300+ players every week in Aberdeen. It's not magic. It's just consistent effort, smart decisions, and a genuine love for coaching.
